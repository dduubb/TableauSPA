Property owners have the right to understand how their tax dollars are used.

Search for your Name, Address or PIN (available on your tax bill) to see how your latest tax bill changed.

We will show you a list of the taxing agencies (schools, libraries, police, parks and many more) in your neighborhood and the ones that had the largest effect on the bill. Many factors influence your tax bill, not only the value of your property but also the value of all of the properties around you and the budget requests of the agencies that provide the services that we all use and create value for you and your neighbors' homes and businesses.

You might be wondering why these changes can vary greatly? This might seem confusing at first, but it's all about complex interplay of reassessments, appeals, and the distribution of the tax burden across different types of properties.

Taxing Agency Changes: The amount of money that needs to be collected from property taxes (the levy) typically doesn't decrease just because property values do. Local governments still need a certain amount of money to provide services.

Your Bill changes: As a homeowner, even if the value of your home hasn't increased, you might find that your property tax bill goes up because of the reduced contributions from commercial properties. On top of that, if you're not aware of or you're not claiming all the tax exemptions you're eligible for, you're missing out on potential savings. It’s like leaving money on the table that could otherwise lower your bill. For example, if there’s a homestead exemption that reduces the taxable value of your home and you don't apply for it, you'll end up with a higher tax bill than necessary. So it's important to check with the Assessor's Office to see if you qualify for any exemptions to reduce your share of the tax burden.

Shift in Tax Burden: If commercial properties are paying less due to lower values and successful appeals, but the total amount of money needed hasn't changed, the tax burden shifts to those whose property values haven't decreased as much. This often means residential property owners.

In short, as we have seen when commercial property values decrease more than residential ones, and if commercial owners are more successful in appealing their property taxes, homeowners may see an increase in their property tax bills at a rate that is not tied to their budges to ensure the total money needed by the local government is still collected.

Map Explainer

The map provided shows the areas covered by different taxing agencies in your region. By moving your cursor over the map, you can see specific information about each agency, like your local school district or the fire department. This helps you see where your property tax dollars are going. As you look at the map and the information on each agency, think about the increases in your tax bill. This map will show you each of the agencies that are funded by your taxes and might help explain why you're seeing changes in your property tax bill.

Pie Explainer

The pie chart you see breaks down how your property tax is divided among the local taxing agencies. Each slice of the pie represents a different agency, like the school district, the police department, or public works. The size of each slice shows how much of your tax money goes to that agency. By looking at the pie chart, you can understand how much of your tax payment is allocated to each service in your community. This visual can help make sense of how the overall tax increases are shared out and why your bill might change. Keep in mind those bigger or smaller slices when you remember the changes in your tax bill.
